Update [me]

Feb. 24th, 2022 05:10 am
siderea
Things is kind of okay. I'm trying to be in low gear (in the idiomatic sense, which is really more like high gear on a motor) for a little bit and focus on self-care. I'm sitting here feeling somewhat accomplished and pleased, basking in the shade of my towering to-do list trying not to look at it too hard.

I've been hanging out a lot in clinical spaces on-line, which I've really been enjoying. Recently I had something sort of like a bucket-list opportunity, where a physician in family medicine wandered into a discussion and asked whether anybody there had any insight on a topic of differential diagnosis of certain psychiatric conditions that I've been molar-grindingly wishing for an opportunity to lecture anybody in primary care on for years, and was all like, "Hold my beer." Long story short I am now getting to tell some physicians how to practice medicine, which is hilarious and they seem to really like it and say they're benefiting by it. I'm glad to get it off my chest.

As a side note! While doing that, I discovered something. I was doing a bit of research to make sure I was right about something, and my google search turned up the original research introducing the PHQ-9, and extremely popular depression screening questionaire in primary care. For professional reasons, I loathe the use of the PHQ-9 and every other so-called depression screening tool I've ever seen or heard of being handed out in primary care. The PHQ-9 – and the HAM-D and the BDI – cannot possibly function correctly used this way. It is a logical impossibility. You cannot diagnose depression by detecting depression, because depression is a component in a bunch of other disorders and conditions, starting with, most famously, Bipolar I and II. Any screen for depression symptoms that doesn't also screen for the symptoms and circumstances of the other things that cause depression cannot possibly tell you that all the patient has is depression. And I always figured that the way primary care uses these things, as, gods help us, conclusively diagnostic for depression, was an abuse of psychiatric tools and probably somehow the fault of the Employee Retirement Income Security Act of 1974 as so many things are, BUT NO!

Turns out that the PHQ-9 was developed by Bob Spitzer (previously) and some other person who is also allegedly a psychiatrist and therefore both of these people should absolutely have known better, and these two sons of bitches actually created this abomination for the purpose of diagnosing depression in primary care. WHAT THE ACTUAL FUCK.

BEHOLD YOUR "EVIDENCE-BASED PRACTICE".

(This isn't even the thing I wanted to rant to PCPs about.)

Effective immediately I am assuming anyone who creates a "screening tool" for any Axis I disorder is a grifter. I'll give the BASIS-32 team a pass because the BASIS-32 isn't disorder specific and does not purport to be diagnostic.

As far as I can tell – and I am open to argument here if somebody actually wants to make a case – here is zero utility in any screen for any specific Axis I disorder in the DSM because the whole damn point of the DSM is to present the definitional criteria sets – the Feighner criteria – that determine, authoritatively, whether or not someone qualifies as having a disorder. You do not need some other set of questions. You can just ask the patient if they have the symptoms listed in the book.

I don't use a depression screen instrument, I literally just photocopy the major depressive episode (now MDD) criteria page out of the DSM and annotate the photocopy as I discuss it with the patient. WHY WOULD ANYONE NEED SOMETHING NOT THE DSM TO DETERMINE WHETHER SOMEONE MEETS THE CRITERIA IN THE DSM BY ASKING THEM QUESTIONS ABOUT THINGS NOT IN THE DSM?

I mean, I always understood the Beck Depression Inventory to be a political tool: Beck's Cognitive Therapy (CT, which is what has largely been retconned to be CBT) looks a lot more effective if you use Beck's idea of what depression should have been defined as instead of what's in the DSM. Beck would very much like everyone to use a yardstick for measuring depression that disproportionately measures what Beck's CT treats. This may be unfair of me, since, given how far back Beck developed CT, it's entirely possible that his (implicit?) criteria for depression are older than those in the DSM (IIRC, published in DSM-III, in 1980). I'm not sure on the timeline here.

This all makes me wonder – as does some of the other things said by the primary care clinicians I'm explaining some psychiatry to – do PCPs not know about the DSM and what's in it? I've gotten at least one question that blew my mind a little because it was a very basic question they could have self-serve answered by looking it up in the DSM, but they didn't seem to know that that is what the DSM has in it.

I've also had a nice discussion about labor organizing and medical professionals. (Go Stanford CIR/SEIU! Solidarity forever!) I'm also having some pleasant extremely technical discussions with fellow therapists about psychotherapeutic theory and practice.

In other news, I am exercising. I'm not great at the regularity thing yet, but it's not nothing either. I got a gizmo that's like the minimum necessary parts of a stationary bike to function and as such it folds up small enough to fit in a box or drawer when not in use, and thus I can fit it in my space with me.
The ergonomics are terrible, but I haven't injured myself yet. My knees seem to like it, and it's improving my conditioning. It turns out that if I can exercise inside my apartment, I am approximately infinitely more likely to do it.

I am also sometimes practicing harp.

A few months ago I got my first menstrual cup, and I've been trialing it. I now probably have a rant coming about that.

I've been working on some Siderea Posts. Hopefully soon.
