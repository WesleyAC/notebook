The Therapists People on Medicare Can't See [healthcare, US, Patreon]

Feb. 16th, 2022 05:57 am
siderea
Canonical link: https://siderea.dreamwidth.org/1751417.html

Did you know there are about two-hundred thousand psychotherapists in the US that people on Medicare can't see?

Medicare – you remember Medicare? As in Medicare-for-All? That Medicare? The one for, primarily, old people? – simply doesn't recognize two of the license types of psychotherapists in the US. That of the mental health counselors, such as the LMHC – which is what I have - and that of the Marriage and Family Therapists, the LMFT.

LMHCs and LMFTs can't "take Medicare" even if we wanted to because as far as Medicare is concerned, we pretty much don't exist. Or rather our licenses don't. So people on Medicare can't see us – not if they want to use their insurance.

And apparently we add up to about 200,000 of the therapists in the US.

I got that number from my professional organization, AMHCA, which – full disclosure – put me up to this. They've asked all members – and, really, anybody who cares about this – to do what they can today in a national day of action, to get word out about this issue.

AMHCA, the American Mental Health Counselors Association, and its membership are not without a financial interest in the matter. Licensed Mental Health Counselors (in all our various names and flavors such as LPC and LCMHC and so forth and so on) would, generally, collectively like to be able to take Medicare.

I think it's because they're slightly dim, or maybe masochistic in the unsexy way, or maybe have a martyrdom complex. Because I have to say, getting in bed with Medicare sounds to me a lot like volunteering to put your hand in the monster's mouth.

Theoretically, as an LMHC, I stand to benefit by being "able" to "take Medicare" if this were to change, which: no fucking thank you. I do not need the "benefit" of "getting" to do a ton of paperwork and submit to burdensome oversight for the pleasure of getting paid way less than my current rates for patients I haven't anywhere on my schedule to put by an un-sue-able payer. I'm good, thanks.

And there is some sort of prestige bump my credential might enjoy, or so some of my colleagues seem to think, if this extremely dubious honor were to befall our profession. I'm an alum of a night school with the cheapest graduate credits in Massachusetts: prestige is really not my thing.

From my perspective, I already can see Medicare clients. Since I don't take any insurance and must be paid cash on the barrel-head, Medicare patients can see me just as much as a patient with any other insurance can. And they can use their insurance to pay me just as much as patients with any other insurance can: not at all.

In fact, if this were to change, and LMHCs (and LMFTs) became able to take Medicare, it would almost certainly make my life materially worse, because there's a lot of nonsense that goes along with a profession being able to take Medicare, and I anticipate that nonsense will get even worse, and it devolves upon every provider with a license that can take Medicare, even if they don't opt to take Medicare. I, personally, selfishly, would really rather that not happen to me.

But.

Apparently there are LMHCs and LMFTs who actually want to be able to take Medicare. I question their life choices, but who am I to obstruct their folly? My alma mater has a specialty program in geropsychotherapy – literally specializing in psychotherapy for the elderly – and not one graduate of that program qualifies to take the insurance the overwhelmingly vast majority of elderly people have because of this situation. I confess I would like to see my colleagues get what they want and be able to do the work they trained to do.

And there are weird negative consequences for LMHCs and LMFTs, especially for ones early in their careers. There are organizations that won't hire LMHCs and LMFTs, full stop, because their work can't be billed directly to Medicare.

Now there's a way to bill the work of an unrecognized provider to Medicare, called "incident to" billing. The idea is that if the psychotherapy is provided "incident to" some other healthcare – such a psychiatric medication management – where the psychotherapist is conceptualized as working as the Medicare-recognized provider's assistant, then that Medicare-recognized provider supervising the psychotherapist can bill Medicare for their assistant's (the psychotherapist's) work. Medicare pays them for the psychotherapist's work, and they can then turn around and pay the psychotherapist. And there are organizations that do this. I worked for one, and saw Medicare patients. It sucked, because there are a whole bunch of rules that apply to Incident To billing which are insane and basically seem punitive to the patient. But many organizations look at the almighty hassle of Incident To billing and go, "You know what? Never mind. We just won't ever hire those people." They could hire them and just never assign them Medicare patients, but even that's too much hassle. They just systematically refuse to hire LMHCs and LMFTs.

So there's workplaces that LMHCs and LMFTs can't get jobs – jobs for which they are otherwise fully qualified – for this reason only. And that can be really hard on the LMHCs and LMFTs who don't want or aren't yet ready to be in private practice. It constrains their employment options.

And that pisses me off on behalf of my colleagues. Particularly on behalf of those trapped in shitty abusive workplaces who really are desperate to get a job someplace else.

So my attitude heretofore has been one of stepping aside. Medicare recognizing the LMHC and the LMFT is not in my personal best interests, but clearly a bunch of my colleagues see it as in their best interests. Far be it for me to be an obstacle to their flourishing. If they want Medicare recognition, let them pursue Medicare recognition.

But.

There are two hundred thousand therapists in the US that elderly people can't see. Because they're elderly.

Congress, by failing to rectify this, has basically walled off a huge percentage of the therapists in the US from people on Medicare who want therapists.

Anybody on Medicare who wants to find a therapist who takes their insurance is starting from a pool with two hundred thousand fewer therapists in it than everybody else in the US.

God damn.

Let us be really clear what we're talking about here. Because women live, on average, longer than men, Medicare beneficiaries are predominantly women. They are predominantly widows.

We are talking about a population that is disproportionately female, disproportionately poor, disproportionately bereft, disproportionately socially isolated, disproportionately disabled and chronically ill, disproportionately prone to psychiatric illness, particularly senile dementia, and almost entirely elderly.

And that is who is having their access to therapy curtailed.

So, yeah, actually, I do have a side in this fight.

Their access curtailed? Oh yes.

See, Medicare is a Federal program. It, unlike Medicaid, the program for poor people run by the individual States, is run by the Federal government. Pretty much everything about Medicare is established by Federal law.

In other words, by Congress.

Medicare recognition of LMHCs and LMFTs would take a literal act of Congress to happen.

(Actually, more than one. But the other already passed.)

And there are members of Congress who do not want to see this happen.

Like I'm pretty sure I've said before, to an insurance company, the very best psychotherapy session is the psychotherapy session that never happened, because then they don't have to pay for it. Anything they can do to make it hard for their insureds to actually get a therapist, and get to see the therapist they have, reduces how much the insurance company ultimately has to pay for therapy, and is therefore in their financial interests, as they see them.

Now they might prefer to just not offer coverage for psychotherapy, but depending where you are in the US, this is more or less illegal, due to what are called parity laws, and also the ACA, which defines a Fully Qualified Health Plan to be one that includes, among other things, mental health care.

So an insurer can't just not cover therapy (probably). So what they do instead is engage in various shenanigans to make it hard for their insureds to actually access the benefits they're supposedly entitled to get under their coverage. For instance if you ever tried to use an insurance company website to find a therapist and wondered why it was so hard to use and had so many listings for therapists that then turned out to not be taking patients, or have moved away, or quit being a therapist entirely, or died: well, right. It's not in the insurer's best interests for you to be able to find a therapist to take your insurance to treat you. Their website sucking is actually a feature for them, economically.

And if you have ever wondered why your insurance seems to have so few therapists that take it, well, it's in the insurance company's financial interests to keep the pool of providers who take their insurance small. If there's not enough of them to go around for every insured who wants a therapist, finding a therapist becomes a game of Musical Chairs where some random bastard who wants a therapist, maybe you, always is the odd one out who can't find one.

Well when it comes to Medicare, really Medicare in an insurance product offered by the Congress of the United States. In this scenario, Congress is the insurance company. And the very same economic incentives that cause insurance companies to get up to shenanigans to frustrate efforts of their beneficiaries to find therapists apply to Congress.

Congress never made a decision to exclude LMHCs and LMFTs. The licenses were invented and proliferated over the last 50 years. Congress just never... got around to doing anything about it.

But some members of Congress – "fiscal conservatives" – have expressed concerns that if Congress recognizes LMHCs and LMFTs, then, well, won't a lot more people on Medicare be able to get therapists? Won't that cost a lot of money?

And apparently – to hear tell from someone in AMHCA who works on lobbying on this issue – they actually have said those things out of the very same mouths they presumably use to kiss their mothers. Unbelievable.

The licensed psychotherapist workforce in the US consists predominantly of psychologists, social workers, and LMHCs and LMFTs. There are other credentials but my impression is that they have very small numbers, comparatively.

According to the American Psychological Association in 2016, there were about 106,000 licensed psychologists in the US. (Source) It is unclear what percentage of that is clinical and counseling psychologists – ones who treat patients – as opposed to scientists and professors.

According to the National Association of Social Workers as of 2017, there are about 350,000 licensed social workers in the US. (Source [PDF]) It's unclear how many of those work as therapists, as opposed to other types of social work in hospitals, schools, and government agencies such as child protective services. This page from the US Bureau of Labor Statistics says that as of 2016 about 130,000 social workers worked in "mental health and substance abuse", irrespective of license. It anticipates that number will climb to about 150,000 by 2026.

So if we assume that there are 120,000 licensed psychologists in 2022 and all of them treat patients, and that there are 150,000 social workers all of whom treat patients, then the 200,000 LMHCs and LMFTs constitute more than a third of the therapist workforce.

Theoretically, if LMHCs and LMFTs were to sign up to take Medicare at the same rate as psychologists and social workers do, then letting them do that would increase the number of psychotherapists available to the elderly by more than fifty percent.

Congress failing to act on this keeps Medicare beneficiaries from accessing – if my numbers are right – more than a third of American therapists.

So, yeah, the "fiscal conservatives" are probably right that that would cost Congress more to pay for all the extra psychotherapy that Medicare beneficiaries might be able to avail themselves of if they suddenly had access to another 50% as many therapists.

To which I can only say: COUGH IT UP, YOU FUCKING GHOULS. STOP TRYING TO SAVE MONEY BY DENYING ELDERLY WIDOWS ACCESS TO THE GRIEF COUNSELING THEY'RE ENTITLED TO BY LAW OH MY GOD.

And that is why I am posting this in my support of bipartisan Senate bill 828, the Mental Health Access Improvement Act, which will recognize LMHCs and LMFTs for Medicare.

Things you can do to help get this passed:

1. Please call or email or @ your Senator! Ask them to support Senate Bill 828, and be sure to use that number to be specific. If you @ your Senator publicly on Twitter or Facebook, please use the hashtags #MedicareMentalHealth and #SeniorsMentalHealth to raise the profile of this issue.

Not sure who your Senator is? AMHCA's got you! See https://www.amhca.org/advocacy/medicare They also have talking points, scripts, and various suggestions if you feel you don't know what to say.

2. Please talk this up on social media. People don't know – Medicare beneficiaries don't know! – that Congress has through neglect and shiftiness cut them off from over a third of working psychotherapists. And some members of Congress are trying to keep doing that just to save a buck.

This is so obviously a social justice issue. A vulnerable population is having their insurance coverage effectively sabotaged by manipulation of the provider pool, to save the insurer money. If it weren't the Federal government doing it – if it were any other insurance company pulling this – it might even be actionable under ERISA as a breach of fiduciary duty.

And it has ramifications for Medicare for All, of course. Sure would be nice if that ever happens, that it doesn't immediately make a third of all therapists no longer covered by insurance. Let's end this here before it goes on a moment longer, because we don't know where Medicare is going.

So many people sneer at "keyboard activitism" as fruitless, but right now that's exactly what this issue needs. It's an issue that very few people know about. Even the people it directly hurts. Raising awareness of this issue is so important, and so helpful. Educating others about this issue is something you can do that does real good.

Again, for posting on the Birbsite or the Book of Face, please use hashtags #MedicareMentalHealth and #SeniorsMentalHealth to raise the profile of this issue. And of course, feel free to forward this post if you think it would help!

3. If you're convinced of the merits of the cause, please ask the people you know and feel comfortable asking such a thing of to contact their Senators, too!
