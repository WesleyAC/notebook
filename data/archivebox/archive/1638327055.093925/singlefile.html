Deciding it is Over, Or, Endemicity [COVID-19, Anthro, Patreon]

Nov. 17th, 2021 01:49 am
siderea
Canonical link: https://siderea.dreamwidth.org/1729719.html

When is a pandemic over? Well, by one measure, when people feel it's not a pandemic any more. Pandemics may have objective criteria and referents, but the concept is nevertheless an abstraction ultimately socially constructed. A pandemic is what we (whether the populace or certain authorities) declare a pandemic, so a pandemic can also be undeclared – regardless of what the germs are up to.

I am seeing lots of little evidence that across the Blue parts of the US, the starlings of attitude are beginning to swoop and seethe in the sky together, surging in the general direction of declaring the pandemic over.

Now, this is happening at the same time various authorities are warning that we don't know what the winter months in the northern hemisphere will hold, meaning that we absolutely do know that they will hold several important holidays wherein people congregate in great numbers and breathe on one another, low temperatures, low relative humidity, less sunlight, more time in poorly ventilated indoor spaces, and increased inebriation – all of which bode poorly for containing COVID.

And there's reports of rising hospitalizations in the US, including here in very vaccinated Massachusetts. I hear Europe is kind of a mess right now.

But there's been a vaccine approved for children as young as 5 years old, and I think that that, along with the intransigence of the militant anti-vaxxers, has, at least in the US, finally tipped the scales for a lot of the Blue Nation. It has been running out of patience.

The LA Times (2021 Nov 12) is now reporting, "CDC shifts pandemic goals away from reaching herd immunity". That's its title. It is not reporting on a specific announcement, so much as an observation they're making about changing messaging from the CDC:
Since the earliest days of the pandemic, there has been one collective goal for bringing it to an end: achieving herd immunity. [...]

Now the herd is restless. And experts at the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention have set aside herd immunity as a national goal. [...]

“Thinking that we’ll be able to achieve some kind of threshold where there’ll be no more transmission of infections may not be possible,” Jones acknowledged last week to members of a panel that advises the CDC on vaccines. [...]

“We would discourage” thinking in terms of “a strict goal,” he said.
What's most interesting is not anything in the article, but how it's being taken by others: as evidence the CDC is moving towards declaring it an endemic illness.

Endemic, here, is construed to mean an exclusive alternative to pandemic. Pandemic doesn't just mean "it's everywhere", it means "and it's an emergency". Endemic means "it's everywhere, and it's not an emergency." Endemic means "here to stay". Endemic means "I guess we just live with it". Endemic means "this is just your life now."

In that sense, endemic is the exit ramp from pandemic. Endemic is what happens after the pandemic is over. Endemic is the end of the pandemic.

I am not yet convinced that's what the CDC is actually saying, at least not yet, but I am increasingly convinced that's what an awful lot of people want to believe the CDC is saying.

I think the public sentiment among the pro-vaccine Americans at this point is that clearly community immunity is impossible, given the chuckleheads we share a country with, so, now that families can mostly protect themselves, fuck it: the pandemic part of the pandemic is done, COVID's just an endemic disease we now have to live with. The human condition has changed, by the addition of one more potentially lethal illness, which you should choose to protect yourself against by getting your shots, which will probably work, presuming you're not one of the immunocompromised it doesn't work for, in which case it's been good to know you.

I'm not saying I disagree with this or that I agree with it. I neither approve nor disapprove. I merely note this to you as a thing that's happening.

There are certainly merits to it as a position. It's mostly got a reality orientation. It does ignore that the second reason for caring about the disease burden in the larger society – after concern about one's self or valued others catching a potentially deadly or crippling disease – is what it does to the hospitals and the medical system. Remember, "flattening the curve"? Having COVID running around loose, even in a substantially vaccinated population, means increasing the burden on the healthcare system by however many additional people get COVID badly enough to need its services.

And I recently read a thought-provoking comment that suggested that what is currently devastating hospitals is actually just the Beer Game: that hospitals are confronting a one-time 10% bump-up in utilization, and because they run so close to their margins, they can't cope with it. The comment also pointed out a fact that was much in discussion in March and April 2020, about which I've heard nothing since: COVID patients on ventilators are typically on them much, much longer than the usual ventilator patient – about three times as long – so, over time, COVID ICU patients absorb much more ICU resources than a typical ICU patient. The beds turn over a third as fast when COVID patients are in them. So daily hospital headcount is misleading: a COVID patient in the ICU longitudinally counts for three patients. Once a hospital ICU is full of COVID patients, it only takes as third as many COVID patients as more typical ICU patients to keep it locked up.

And, of course, the reason to flatten the curve isn't just for the patients with COVID, themselves, but for everyone else who requires hospital care or emergency care: for everyone mangled in car accidents, for people with cancer, for people who have heart attacks and strokes and kidney failure, for people who come down with all the other interesting infectious diseases which could kill them, and all the rest. There are people who have died because of COVID of things other than COVID, because they couldn't get the medical care they needed, because people with COVID needed that medical care and there wasn't enough to go around.

These things suggest regarding COVID as no longer an emergency, which is to say not a public policy problem, is unwise.

But... this is America. Being unwise about the public health consequences of access to healthcare is kind of our brand.

We have shown – for long time before the pandemic – how little we care about our healthcare system, and how little we expect of it, and how little we think of it as a mete subject for public policy consideration.

Constant readers with a long memory may recall that this is how I got on the topics of pandemics in the first place back in 2018: a conversation I had with someone wherein they asked why it was that here in the US you don't (most places) have to pay to have the fire department rush to your house to put out a fire, but you do have to pay to have an ambulance rush to your house to transport you to an emergency department. The answer being that emergency fire response was put together by cities because fire was rightly understood as a threat to the whole city, while medical problems were and are conceptualized as merely private woes never a threat to one's neighbors or city or country. Our entire medical system, in how it is organized and situated in American society, is predicated on the notion that contagious diseases don't exist.

I'm not suggesting this is good, but there's something grimly realistic about looking at overwhelmed hospitals and shrugging. Yeah, we could care about that, I guess; it's not like we were caring about that before the pandemic. We were already in trouble, as a society, and we weren't doing anything about it then, so... this is just a more severe version of our old normal.

I mean, while all this has been going on, Massachusetts lost a hospital – with, yes, an ED – to climate catastrophe and... I guess we've done nothing about it? Norwood Hospital is still closed. I know of no timeline to reopen it. Likewise, back in 2019 (check out that timing!) Partners decided, over local objections, to shut down Union Hospital in Lynn by 2020, and divert ambulances to the ED at Salem Hospital, instead. This struck at least me as horrifying, because while I'm sure it's clinically lovely, Salem Hospital is, like it says on the tin, in Salem, a municipality famous outside of Massachusetts for witchcraft and inside of Massachusetts for traffic. Salem is not on any highway, and has no meaningful main thoroughfares. Roads in Salem are narrow and inadequate for regular commuting; during its month-long Halloween silly season Salem has impassible congestion on roads where there is no place to pull over. Sending ambulances from the communities Union Hospital's ED served to Salem means some inevitable number of stroke patients dying in the backs of ambulances.

And this is in Massachusetts! Where we allegedly care about healthcare and medicine and public health and stuff. Comparatively a lot, or so I'm told, for a part of the US!

This is who we are. We didn't care about keeping our hospitals open even when we didn't have a pandemic. We didn't care about ED boarding then, either. We didn't care about there not being enough inpatient psychiatric beds or outpatient psychiatrists. We called the proliferation of opioid dependence and concomitant overdose an "epidemic", but we didn't do anything to make medication assisted treatment (MAT) more available. We, as a society, didn't fix those problems. We didn't even really think of them a public policy problems. And we certainly didn't think of them as emergencies. And if we didn't think of those things as emergencies, why would we think of hospitals being overwhelmed due to COVID as an emergency? Why would we think of anything as an emergency? Do we even believe in the idea of emergencies when it comes to the state and availability of health care? Do we grant that there is such a thing, ever, as a healthcare access emergency, or is that just not a thing?

In the US, where medical care is a commodity bought and sold on the open market, we're already used to the idea that sometimes people just don't get medical care they need because they can't pay for it. Since we're already accustomed to thinking of medical care in those terms, thinking of it as "available while supplies last" and first-come-first serve, festival seating aren't any sort of stretch at all.

Americans are so used to health care just not being there for them when they need it, it seems, yes, a little precious to imagine them starting to trouble about that now. We're already used to eight hour, twelve hour, twenty-four hour waits in emergency rooms, we're already used to multi-month and half-year waits for outpatient specialists, we're already used to people avoiding hospitals until in medical crisis because of the fear of the bill, we're already used to harried and overworked clinicians not having the time of day for us. How different is this, really? Or at least, how different does it sound?

We, as a society, haven't cared about the unavailability of hospital care in general. Is there any point in expecting us to start now?

And we, as a society, have certainly not cared about what happens to the people who work in these systems. If our health care systems were exploitative of labor, often abusive or dangerous places to work – which they were – that was not considered a matter for the public. We had no sympathy for the effect on those people as people, and we didn't even have the enlightened self-interest to consider what that meant for patient care. We didn't consider it a threat to public health to have residents working 18 hour shifts, so exhausted they can't focus their eyes well enough to read the charts; we didn't consider it a threat to public health to allow inpatient nurses to be assigned absolutely any number of patients at a time. We didn't consider that an emergency, or even important, and we did nothing about it. For literal decades, we did nothing much about the inadequate numbers of doctors and other treaters for the populations they served, nor about the insurance systems' criminal withholding of or thwarting access to medical care.

There's something, sad to say, grimly realistic about shrugging and saying: given that we Americans basically consider being unable to access medical care a reasonable thing that normally happens here, it doesn't seem reasonable to expect Americans to be willing to make any further personal sacrifices to forfend that fate. There is very little point in expecting otherwise. This is what we picked for ourselves, as a society, well before the pandemic. This is the normal we picked for our society. If the pandemic makes these things more common and more severe, well, it's a change of degree, not of kind.

I think maybe Americans are done flattening the curve.

And in any event, then there's the deer. COVID is now very firmly established in a vigorous mammalian species with a continent-wide range besides us: the white-tailed deer:
Scientists have evidence that SARS-CoV-2 spreads explosively in white-tailed deer and that the virus is widespread in this deer population across the United States. [...]

Since SARS-CoV-2, the coronavirus that causes the disease COVID-19, first emerged, there have been several signs that white-tailed deer would be highly susceptible to the virus — and that many of these animals were catching it across the country.

In September of last year, computer models suggested SARS-CoV-2 could easily bind to and enter the deer's cells. A recent survey of white-tailed deer in the Northeast and Midwest found that 40% of them had antibodies against SARS-CoV-2.

Now veterinarians at Pennsylvania State University have found active SARS-CoV-2 infections in at least 30% of deer tested across Iowa during 2020. Their study, published online last week, suggests that white-tailed deer could become what's known as a reservoir for SARS-CoV-2.
Worse, study of the strains infecting them have proved that, bogglingly, somehow they're managing to get infected from humans. Repeatedly.
During this time frame, the team also sequenced the genes of nearly 100 samples of the virus. They found the variants circulating in the deer matched the variants circulating in people.

Those genomic sequences suggest that during the pandemic, deer have caught the virus from people multiple times in Iowa alone, Kapur says. "The data are very consistent again with frequent spillover events from humans into deer and then transmission among the animals."
They're not having a separate, parallel pandemic springing from one cervid patient zero who caught it from a human. They're participating in our pandemic. Somehow.

Everything about this is completely terrible. I'm pretty sure deer don't live in enclosed spaces, so they're managing to pass it around out of doors; apparently it's not very lethal to them, so they're very able to pass it on; and given the amazing prevalence rate among wild deer, it would seem they are super susceptible to catching it. And somehow, they're having enough contact with humans to catch it from us, which may mean they have enough contact with us to give it back to us, if it turns out we can catch it from them. Whether we can is as of right now unknown, but it's not unlikely.
Their study, published online last week, suggests that white-tailed deer could become what's known as a reservoir for SARS-CoV-2. That is, the animals could carry the virus indefinitely and spread it back to humans periodically.

If that's the case, it would essentially dash any hopes of eliminating or eradicating the virus in the U.S. — and therefore from the world — says veterinary virologist Suresh Kuchipudi at Penn State, who co-led the study.

"If the virus has opportunities to find an alternate host besides humans, which we would call a reservoir, that will create a safe haven where the virus can continue to circulate even if the entire human population becomes immune," he says. "And so it becomes more and more complicated to manage or even eradicate the virus."
So there's now another zoonotic reservoir in which it can spread unchecked. A really big one. The article says there are estimated to be about 30 million white-tail deer in the US. I don't think we have any plans to vaccinate them all.

All of which mostly likely means COVID is never, ever going away.

As to where we stand with the disease itself and our ability to manage it, I suspect this is about as good as it's going to get. The risk of COVID will not be much further mitigated, at least not for a long time; the risk of infection may increase with mutation, but will probably be managed by interventions which keep it mostly sublethal; we'll probably all be getting boosters for the rest of our lives, or until an even better new vaccine is invented.

In embracing endemicity, I suspect COVID will become to us not unlike what consumption – tuberculosis - was to the 18th and 19th centuries. Much less lethal, to be sure, but: the ubiquitous, ordinary killer always in the background while society, there being nothing more to be done about it, necessarily just got on with things. As the White Plague was just the thing they had to deal with, perhaps COVID is just the thing we will have to deal with.

Which is not to say we will be giving up all efforts to mitigate transmission. Quite to the contrary, it seems to me that something that is happening to us is that we're learning to believe in respiratory viruses. Oh, we, as a society, knew about respiratory viruses, but we didn't really believe in them. We learned about them from classrooms learning and science shows on TV and books and movies, and then turned around and acted like they don't exist.

We don't do that any more. We actually think about the air we breathe and where it's been. We had been sucking down all sorts of infectious nastiness and being casual about causality – "Oh, shucks, I caught a bug" – and I don't think we can do that any more. Masks work. Social distancing works. Catching upper respiratory infections feels a lot less like an act of God than something one should take some personal responsibility in preventing.

As part of that new awareness, there is an awareness of risks in general, beyond COVID, because we cannot reckon what the risks of COVID mean to us without considering the larger context of what other risks mean to us, and that just forces us to ask what those other risks are. What, exactly, are the risks of getting influenza? What are the chances of getting infected, and what are the chances of getting really sick? Of getting permanent illness? Of dying? What about the risks of all the other infectious respiratory illnesses out there?

The possibility of long COVID just illuminates the possibility of having protracted debilitating symptoms from other infectious respiratory illnesses. In a sense, the specter of long COVID has forced the larger society to entertain the possibility that the graveyard it has been whistling past, of reports of long syndromes after other infectious illnesses and of the possibility that other chronic illnesses are caused by as-of-yet unnoted respiratory infections, is very real and very haunted. It forces us to entertain the horrible possibility that maybe catching whatever is going around is not without serious potential consequences we as a society haven't wholly reckoned. Maybe there are risks we haven't properly appreciated, because we never took seriously they were possible.

So that, too, is endemicity. Not giving up avoidance and mitigation as futile, but accepting some measure of them as routine, and weaving them into our culture.
